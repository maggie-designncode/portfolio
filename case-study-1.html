Background

The platform, the team, the opportunity

Execute is JPMorgan's single-dealer platform where institutional clients trade macro products. I work as part of a small three-person design team. Historically, Execute has focused on the client-facing side — but a growing push to support internal sales workflows too created an opportunity to unify both experiences under a shared design system.

This project was my focus for about a year: building the first client-facing electronic trading tool for securitized products on Execute — a category that had never been digitized at this scale.

Discovery

Starting with more questions than answers

The business goal was broadly understood from the start: electronify the manual, high-stakes workflows in the securitized product group (SPG). But "broadly understood" meant almost nothing was actually defined. When I joined the project, I came in with more questions than answers.

What are we actually building? For whom? Which products? And what's the workflow we're trying to replace?

These weren't rhetorical questions — they were the entire first phase of my work. I started by conducting stakeholder interviews with traders, salespeople, and internal partners to map the current landscape before touching any design tools.

Scope

Narrowing in on the right problem

SPG covers a wide range of financial products, but almost all of them trade through just two mechanisms: TBA (To Be Announced) and BWIC (Bid Wanted in Competition). Most SPG products follow a relatively predictable flow — clients receive lists, request quotes via Bloomberg, and sales books the trade. Straightforward enough to not need our attention.

Two mechanisms stood out as genuinely high-friction: TBA instruments and BWICs. But they weren't equal opportunities.

TBA is standardized and already well-supported by platforms like Tradeweb. BWIC, on the other hand, is a different beast — an auction-based process with fragmented communication, no clean digital solution on the market, and a bidding stage that existing tools handle poorly. On top of that, JPMorgan issues and markets its own BWIC lists, which meant digitizing this flow could directly increase list visibility and drive more client engagement on Execute.

We prioritized BWIC for MVP. Not because it was easier — it wasn't — but because the opportunity was bigger and the gap in the market was real.

Mapping the buy-side client experience

I started by mapping the end-to-end buy-side journey from client interviews and stakeholder conversations. This gave us a shared view of where friction was highest — and surfaced three pain points that would shape the entire project.

Most of the map made sense. Clients were overwhelmed by email, couldn't track bids across tools, and had no centralized view of where they stood. But the third pain point stopped me: winning a bid didn't seem to guarantee the trade was done. That felt important — and I didn't yet understand why.

Research

Going deeper: what the journey map couldn't explain

The initial journey map gave us the client-side view — where friction was, how people felt, which touchpoints were failing them. But the third pain point nagged at me. Clients were uncertain even after winning. Prices moved. Trades didn't close. I wanted to understand exactly what was happening on the other side of that experience — and to what extent design could help.

I tried to get answers through trader interviews, but hit a wall. These are people operating in a high-frequency environment where years of habit have become muscle memory. Asking them to describe a process they run on autopilot wasn't producing the structured picture I needed.

So I shifted. I moved to the engineering team who built internal tools for SPG traders — the people who had mapped the workflow systematically from the inside. As they demoed how traders actually move through their day — negotiating, repricing, executing — I drew the flow diagram live alongside them.

What emerged explained the third pain point completely. BWIC trading isn't linear after a bid is won. Here's what actually happens:

Depending on the product structure, there's often a second round of negotiation after an initial winning bid — buy-side and sell-side re-enter talks to confirm the final price. Even then, the winning client may not reach the final stage. Either the second-round price falls off, or the client may not be cleared to trade from a regulatory standpoint. At that point, the BWIC returns to market and reopens for bidding — if the expiration window hasn't passed. No client is notified. No system tracks it. It just happens, invisibly, in chat.

This wasn't an edge case. It was a structural reality of how these products trade — and nothing in the existing tooling acknowledged it. The client-side journey map I'd built reflected what clients experienced. The engineering interviews revealed why they experienced it.

That gap between the two became the foundation of everything that followed.

01
The second round is informal

After an initial winning bid, there's often a second negotiation round. No tooling structures this — it happens over chat, with no visibility for the client into where they stand or how long it will take.

02
Winning ≠ done

A winning bid can still fall through — either because the second-round price collapses, or because the client fails a regulatory clearance check. When that happens, the BWIC silently returns to market. No one outside the trading desk knows.

How might we...

Structure the second-round negotiation so it's transparent and time-bounded — making an informal, invisible process explicit for both sides?

How might we...

Clearly separate "bid selection" from "irrevocable execution" — preserving flexibility while giving the business the ability to de-risk the fallback scenario?

Define

Redefining the BWIC flow

To account for these realities, we redefined the BWIC workflow into four distinct stages — moving away from the informal, overlapping phases that caused confusion, and toward a structure that could actually be designed against.

Stage 1
Submission
Seller creates and submits the BWIC list
Stage 2
Auction
Dealers bid; seller reviews, negotiates, identifies winner
Stage 3
Finalization
Winning bid verified, final price confirmed
Stage 4
Booking
Trade executed and settled

Design

From insight to interface

With the four-stage BWIC flow defined, the design work moved in two distinct iterations — each one grounded in what we learned from the previous round.

Iteration 1
Building the core workflow
  • Centralized place for browsing and evaluating available bonds
  • End-to-end electronic ticket — from raising a bid to winning or losing
  • Clear, defined workflow statuses at every stage of the BWIC process
Iteration 2
Responding to real user needs
  • Bulk bid submission and management — per direct user request
  • Alerts for newly available bonds — for clients whose attention isn't on Execute full-time

Why Option B

Through conversations with users and proxy users, we discovered that browsing available bond lists and monitoring active bids aren't sequential tasks — they happen at the same time. A trader might be evaluating a new list while simultaneously watching whether a bid they submitted minutes ago is winning or losing. Option A forced them to choose one context or the other. Option B keeps both visible, eliminating a critical blind spot at exactly the moment when speed and awareness matter most.

Design review — what we heard

Formal usability testing wasn't feasible given the access constraints of working within a large financial institution — traders are hard to reach, their schedules are driven by markets, and structured research sessions aren't always possible. Instead, I ran design reviews with three participants across different roles: a CIO/Portfolio Manager, a Director of Specs trading, and an Agency MBS trader.

It wasn't a perfect research process. But it was real feedback from real users — and it surfaced things that changed the product.

Three themes came through clearly:

01
Bulk is the real workflow

Multiple participants bid on 20–30 bonds at a time, often pasting from their own spreadsheets. Single-bond submission wasn't how they actually worked — the design needed to support bulk bidding as a first-class interaction, not an afterthought.

02
Status clarity is everything

Participants were confused by bid status labels and what they meant at each stage. In a high-stakes, time-sensitive environment, ambiguity around "submitted" vs. "countered" vs. "won pending" creates real risk. The language and visual hierarchy needed to be unambiguous.

03
Execute is one of many platforms

Clients are not going to watch Execute all day. They trade competitive prices across platforms — Bloomberg, IB Chat, Tradeweb — and Execute has to earn their attention. If a new BWIC list drops and they miss it, the opportunity is gone.

Iteration 2 — bulk bidding and alerts

The bulk submit request came directly from the review. Traders described pasting bids from their own sheets, bidding on 20+ bonds at once, and needing to manage and update those bids as a group — not one by one. The single-bond flow we built in iteration 1 was correct for the workflow logic, but it didn't match how these users actually operated at scale.

"Execute is a single-dealer platform. Clients are not going to stare at it all day long — they trade competitively across platforms. How might we alert clients about new bonds even when their attention is elsewhere?"

The alerts feature addressed a structural challenge that no amount of good UI could fix on its own: if clients aren't on the platform when a new BWIC list appears, they miss the window entirely. The busiest trading time for specs is 10–11:30am — a tight window where attention is split across multiple systems. Alerts gave Execute a way to reach clients on their terms, rather than expecting them to monitor yet another screen.

Within the first half-year of launch, the product generated $2.4B in trading volume with $1.3B executed — strong early validation that digitizing this workflow created real business value. 37 monthly active users and 45 demos in H1 showed healthy adoption for a zero-to-one product in a space that had never had an electronic trading tool before.

What the design addressed

The split-view layout directly tackled the visibility and fragmentation problems surfaced in the journey map — clients no longer had to choose between browsing available bonds and monitoring in-flight bids. The four-stage BWIC flow gave structure to a process that had previously been informal and undocumented, reducing ambiguity for both clients and the trading desk.

What remains an open question

The operational risk around bid fallback — the scenario where a winning bidder can't fulfill the trade — was a known problem that shaped how we defined the workflow stages. Whether the design fully de-risks this in practice is something I want to revisit with clearer data. This is an area I'd approach differently with more time, and likely an opportunity for a future iteration.
